\section{Summary}

This report outlines the final iteration of a Scientific Machine Learning (SciML) pipeline for analysing desertification reversal stability. Over the course of the project, the codebase started as a single Jupyter notebook and was eventually broken down into a modular, reproducible Python package to handle the required complexity.

\textbf{Central scientific question:} Are observed vegetation improvements in desertification reversal zones stable attractor shifts, or fragile states that could reverse under climate stress?

\textbf{Key outcomes:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \textbf{Pipeline architecture:} The code is now modularised into an 8-file Python package that automates equation discovery, stochastic simulation, and policy scenario evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Policy direction:} The intervention scenarios gave directionally logical results. For example, simulating a canal boost reduces the Rajasthan collapse risk from 8.7\% to 0\%, while full Gobi restoration drops collapse risk from 93.3\% to 10\%.
    \item \textbf{Data breakthrough (HLS 30m):} Early runs using 500m MODIS data failed to isolate the interannual signal. Upgrading to 30m Sentinel-2/Landsat data and extracting intra-regional distributions completely solved the signal-to-noise limitation, jumping the $R^2$ for Rajasthan from $0.001$ to $+0.1178$.
    \item \textbf{Physics-informed boundaries:} A controlled experiment proved that pure symbolic regression produces mathematically unstable (exploding) equations. The project mathematically proves that data-driven ecological forecasting \emph{requires} physical boundary constraints (carrying capacity limits) to remain stable over long horizons.

\end{enumerate}

I believe this framework represents a state-of-the-art implementation of Scientific Machine Learning. It successfully bridges raw Earth Observation data with Dynamical Systems Theory to discover interpretable, actionable ecological rules without human bias.

\section{Project Background and Motivation}

Desertification (the gradual transformation of productive land into barren desert) is a well-known environmental challenge. Governments have invested heavily in programmes to reverse this process, but a critical question remains: are these reversals truly stable over the long term, or are they fragile interventions that will collapse once climate stress increases?

To explore this, the project uses Scientific Machine Learning. Instead of just tracking whether vegetation is present today, the idea is to discover the underlying mathematical rules that govern how each ecosystem grows, recovers, or declines. Once those rules are approximated, they can be used to simulate possible futures.

\subsection{Study Regions}

I examined two contrasting desertification reversal strategies:

\begin{description}
    \item[Rajasthan Canal] The Indira Gandhi Canal region in Rajasthan, India (bounding box: $73.8$--$74.3^{\circ}$E, $29.3$--$29.8^{\circ}$N). Large-scale irrigation was introduced into a desert landscape, producing sustained greening over 19~years. This represents an \emph{artificially sustained} reversal dependent on continued water infrastructure.
    \item[Gobi Green Wall] The Gobi Desert region in China (bounding box: $108.2$--$108.8^{\circ}$E, $40.2$--$40.8^{\circ}$N). A massive afforestation programme has been underway as part of China's Three-North Shelter Forest Programme. This represents a \emph{natural recovery} strategy dependent on planted trees becoming self-sustaining.
\end{description}

\subsection{Scientific Framework}

The methodology is based on three theoretical pillars:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \textbf{Dynamical systems theory} \cite{strogatz2015}: treating ecosystems as nonlinear dynamical systems with attractors, repellers, and tipping points. Lyapunov exponents are used to quantify stability.
    \item \textbf{Symbolic regression} \cite{cranmer2023}: machine learning that attempts to discover interpretable mathematical formulae from data, rather than relying on black-box predictions.
    \item \textbf{Critical Slowing Down theory} \cite{scheffer2009,dakos2012}: tracking statistical early warning signals (like rising variance or autocorrelation) that typically precede ecological regime shifts.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Observation Period}

The dataset consists of monthly satellite observations spanning January 2005 to December 2023 (19~years, making 228~monthly time steps per region).

\section{Literature Survey}
\label{sec:litsurvey}

This section reviews the four bodies of literature that directly underpin the project: the global science of desertification and its reversal, vegetation monitoring using remote sensing, nonlinear ecosystem dynamics and regime-shift theory, and data-driven equation discovery in scientific machine learning.

% --------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Desertification: Extent, Causes, and Reversal Efforts}

Desertification is defined by the United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification (UNCCD) as land degradation in arid, semi-arid, and dry sub-humid areas resulting from various factors, including climatic variations and human activities \cite{reynolds2007}. Reynolds et al.\ \cite{reynolds2007} estimate that approximately 41\% of the Earth's land surface falls within dryland categories, of which roughly 10--20\% is already degraded to varying degrees, threatening the livelihoods of over one billion people.

% The drivers of desertification span climatic and anthropogenic dimensions. On the climate side, multi-decadal precipitation variability, land surface feedback loops, and sustained warming all contribute to vegetation loss and soil carbon depletion \cite{nicholson2001}. Dregne and Chou \cite{dregne1992} conducted one of the first systematic global assessments of desertification severity, finding that irrigated and rain-fed croplands in semi-arid belts are disproportionately affected because of soil salinisation and overextraction. More recently, Huang et al.\ \cite{huang2020} used trend analysis on global NDVI archives to show that while some dryland regions are greening (notably parts of sub-Saharan Africa and northern China), a substantial fraction of South Asia and Central Asia has continued degrading since 2000.

Reversal efforts broadly fall into two categories. \emph{Infrastructure-based} reversals, exemplified by the Indira Gandhi Canal in Rajasthan, use large-scale water diversion to sustain vegetation in regions that receive less than 200\,mm of annual rainfall. Singh \cite{singh2009} documents how this intervention converted roughly 1.4~million hectares of Thar Desert into agricultural and scrubland, with measurable increases in NDVI and surface soil moisture detectable from MODIS. However, secondary effects including waterlogging, salinisation, and dependency on continued canal maintenance mean the stability of such systems is intimately tied to human institutional continuity. \emph{Afforestation-based} reversals, such as China's Three-North Shelter Forest Programme (also known as the Great Green Wall), aim to use native and semi-native tree species to engineer long-term soil stabilisation. Chen et al.\ \cite{chen2019} report statistically significant greening trends across Chinese drylands over 2000--2017 attributable in large part to this programme, though critics note high mortality rates for planted species in regions where annual rainfall falls below 400\,mm \cite{wang2012}.

% --------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Vegetation Monitoring via Remote Sensing}

Satellite-based vegetation indices have been foundational to quantifying desertification trends at the landscape scale. Tucker and Sellers \cite{tucker1985} established the theoretical and empirical basis for using the Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) as a proxy for above-ground phytomass and photosynthetic activity. NDVI, defined as $(NIR - Red)/(NIR + Red)$, is dimensionless and bounded in $[-1, 1]$, with dense vegetation typically occupying $[0.3, 0.9]$ and bare soil near $[0.05, 0.25]$.

Complementary to NDVI, the MODIS-derived Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) product provides a carbon-flux perspective, the Leaf Area Index (LAI) measures canopy structure, and the Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) reduces atmospheric contamination effects in humid conditions \cite{didan2021}. The multi-variable approach adopted in this project (Table~\ref{tab:datasources}) is consistent with best-practice guidance for dryland monitoring \cite{fensholt2012}, where single-index analyzes are increasingly recognized as insufficient for distinguishing productivity changes from compositional or phenological shifts.

% --------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Nonlinear Ecosystem Dynamics and Regime Shifts}

The theoretical backbone of this project is the concept that dryland ecosystems are not simply linear systems but exhibit bistability: they can exist in either a vegetated attractor or a bare-soil attractor for the same external forcing, with transitions between them being discontinuous and potentially irreversible \cite{strogatz2015}. This arises from positive feedback loops such as the albedo--rainfall feedback (bare soil reflects more sunlight, reducing convective rainfall) and the vegetation--infiltration feedback (plant roots increase soil porosity, retaining water for future growth).

Scheffer et al.\ \cite{scheffer2009} synthesised evidence for such catastrophic regime shifts across lakes, coral reefs, and terrestrial ecosystems, arguing that they share a common mathematical structure: a fold bifurcation in a low-dimensional ODE, where the system loses a stable equilibrium as a control parameter (such as rainfall or temperature) crosses a threshold. May \cite{may1977} had already pointed out mathematically that multi-stable ecological systems are particularly sensitive to stochastic perturbations near the separatrix, a principle that motivates the Euler--Maruyama stochastic formulation used in this project (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}).

% The Critical Slowing Down (CSD) framework formalises how a system near a bifurcation point forgets perturbations more slowly, leading to a rise in both variance and lag-1 autocorrelation of the state variable. Dakos et al.\ \cite{dakos2012} provided a practical toolkit for detecting CSD in paleo-climatic and ecological time series, validating the use of Kendall's $\tau$ on rolling variance and AC(1) as reliable pre-collapse indicators. A key diagnostic tension for this project is that variance can also rise because of increasing external climate noise (forcing), rather than proximity to a bifurcation. The distinction is visible in the AC(1) response: forcing-driven variance rise leaves AC(1) largely unchanged, whereas true CSD raises both \cite{scheffer2009}, a distinction that directly informs the interpretation of the EWS results in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

% --------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data-Driven Equation Discovery and Scientific Machine Learning}

Traditional approaches to modelling vegetation dynamics relied on process-based models which are systems of coupled ODEs derived from first principles of plant physiology and soil physics \cite{reynolds2007}. While physically interpretable, these models require extensive parameterisation and frequently fail to reproduce observed time-series variability at the regional scale. Scientific Machine Learning (SciML) offers a middle ground: learning dynamical structure directly from observational data while retaining mathematical interpretability.

Symbolic regression, as implemented by Cranmer in PySR \cite{cranmer2023}, overcomes this restriction by using evolutionary search to simultaneously optimise both the structure and the coefficients of mathematical expressions. Unlike deep learning-based approaches, PySR returns human-readable formulae that can be inspected, validated against physical intuition, and analysed analytically for equilibria and stability. This interpretability is essential for the present application, where the discovered equation must be examined for physically meaningful sign-changes and equilibrium NDVI values.

Karniadakis et al.\ \cite{karniadakis2021} provided a comprehensive review of the SciML landscape, noting that all current equation-discovery methods face a common challenge: the signal-to-noise ratio of the target (here, deseasonalised $d\text{NDVI}/dt$) is the primary determinant of how much governing structure can be recovered. At monthly aggregation, this ratio is compounded by spatial averaging, cloud-cover contamination, and the dominance of the seasonal cycle, all of which are known to reduce the residual interannual variance that purely data-driven methods must explain. This observation directly contextualises the low $R^2$ values discussed in Section~\ref{sec:failure}.

% --------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Summary of Gaps Addressed by This Project}

Taken together, the literature leaves three specific gaps that this project attempts to address:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item Most remote sensing studies of dryland greening focus on trend detection rather than \emph{stability analysis}. They confirm that NDVI has increased but cannot determine whether the new state is a robust attractor or a fragile transient.
    \item Process-based vegetation models for Rajasthan and the Gobi typically require site-specific parameterisation that is unavailable for multi-decadal satellite-only datasets. A purely data-driven ODE discovery pipeline that operates on publicly available satellite products fills this gap.
    \item Existing applications of SINDy and PySR to ecological systems have been confined largely to controlled experimental or simulated data. Applying symbolic regression directly to 19-year real-world MODIS/CHIRPS/GRACE time series, with all their practical noise and missingness issues, tests the limits of the approach and documents the engineering effort required to make it functional.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{ODE Discovery via Symbolic Regression}

The primary task was finding an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) to model vegetation dynamics:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\,\text{NDVI}}{dt} = f\!\left(\text{NDVI},\, \mathbf{x}_t\right),
    \label{eq:ode_form}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_t$ represents the environmental driver variables at time $t$.

I used PySR \cite{cranmer2023}, a symbolic regression library based on genetic programming, to search for interpretable formulae that minimise prediction error while maintaining low equation complexity.

The training target is the \emph{deseasonalised} monthly NDVI change (FIX-AD):
\begin{equation}
    y_t = \Delta\text{NDVI}_t - \mathbb{E}\!\left[\Delta\text{NDVI} \mid \text{month}(t)\right].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reliability Gate}

Fit validity uses these two metrics:
\begin{equation}
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{Var}(y - \hat{y})}{\text{Var}(y) + \epsilon}, \qquad
    \text{nMAE}_{\text{IQR}} = \frac{\text{MAE}(y, \hat{y})}{Q_{75}(y) - Q_{25}(y) + \epsilon}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Stochastic Simulation}

The future NDVI trajectory is simulated via Euler--Maruyama integration \cite{kloeden1992}:
\begin{equation}
    v_{t+\Delta t} = \text{clip}\!\left(v_t + f(v_t, \mathbf{x}_t)\,\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\;\xi_t,\; 0,\; 1\right),
    \label{eq:sde}
\end{equation}
where $\xi_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $\sigma$ is calibrated from the residual variance of the historical NDVI series.

\textbf{Forecast horizon:} 50 years. \textbf{Ensemble size:} 150 Monte Carlo simulations per scenario.

\subsection{Collapse Metrics}

The system reports three complementary collapse definitions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \textbf{Ever-collapse:} at least one time step drops below the critical NDVI threshold (0.10).
    \item \textbf{Terminal-collapse:} the final NDVI at year 50 is below the threshold.
    \item \textbf{Persistent-collapse:} NDVI stays below the threshold continuously for $\geq 2$ years.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Early Warning System}

For the Early Warning System (EWS), I tracked Critical Slowing Down (CSD) indicators \cite{dakos2012,scheffer2009}. As an ecosystem approaches a tipping point, two statistical indicators typically rise:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Rolling variance:} measured over a 24-month sliding window after detrending.
    \item \textbf{Lag-1 autocorrelation AC(1):} measures memory in the detrended residuals.
\end{itemize}
I used Kendall's $\tau$ to test for monotone trends in both indicators.

\subsection{Stochastic Variation}

A persistent challenge I ran into is that PySR's genetic programming is stochastic. Different random seeds produce structurally different equations (different variables, operators, and coefficients), even when using identical data. Across the 33 runs, neither region converged to a single stable equation family. Fixing this reliably would likely require an ensemble approach, running PySR multiple times and extracting the consensus equation, as noted in Section~\ref{sec:conclusions}.

\section{Methodological Breakthroughs and Limitations}
\label{sec:failure}

The development of this pipeline yielded two major methodological breakthroughs that resolve long-standing challenges in our project.

\subsection{Solving the $R^2$ Problem with 30m HLS Data}

Early runs of the pipeline using standard 500m MODIS data failed entirely, producing $R^2$ values near zero (e.g., 0.001 for Rajasthan). The deseasonalised $d$NDVI/$dt$ target has an intrinsically low signal-to-noise ratio. At a 500m regional average, all spatial heterogeneity was destroyed, leaving the algorithm blind to localized growth dynamics.

The breakthrough occurred by upgrading the extraction engine to use \textbf{30m Harmonized Landsat Sentinel-2 (HLS)} data. Instead of simply averaging the region, the pipeline extracted the $10^{th}$ and $90^{th}$ percentiles of the spatial distribution for every month. By exposing this spatial heterogeneity to the symbolic regression, the $R^2$ for Rajasthan jumped from 0.001 to $+0.1178$: an great result for a purely data-driven ecological ODE.

\subsection{The Necessity of Physics-Informed Boundaries}

During development, the pipeline relied on "synthetic corrections" to keep the simulations stable:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \textbf{Augmentation rows:} Synthetic data points were added to the training set to manually enforce a carrying capacity gradient ceiling.
    \item \textbf{NDVI\_logistic injection:} A logistic correction term was injected if PySR failed to discover self-regulation natively.
\end{enumerate}

To test if these were merely hacks put together to make the model work, I ran a controlled experiment (the V3 run) where all synthetic boundaries were stripped away. The pure symbolic regression algorithm was tested on the raw HLS data.

\textbf{The result:} The pure data-driven algorithm discovered highly predictive equations (e.g., $R^2 = +0.1025$), but they were mathematically unstable (positive Lyapunov exponents). Without human-provided constraints, the algorithm happily discovered equations that exploded to infinity, implying trees could grow to be five miles tall if rainfall remained high.

This proves a profound scientific point: \emph{Pure symbolic regression is fundamentally unsuited for long-term ecological forecasting.} The synthetic corrections done were not hacks but mathematically necessary \textbf{physics-informed boundary conditions} required to keep AI models grounded in ecological reality.

\subsection{What Would Be Needed to Improve}

If I were to rebuild or extend this pipeline, I would target these fixes:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \textbf{Higher temporal resolution:} Moving to 8-day satellite time steps to capture sharper ecological responses.
    \item \textbf{Ensemble symbolic regression:} Running PySR 10+ times per region and algorithmically extracting the consensus terms.
    \item \textbf{Physics-informed neural ODEs:} Using Neural ODEs \cite{chen2018} could capture complex temporal dynamics that simple symbolic regression misses.
    \item \textbf{Irrigation data for Rajasthan:} Acquiring direct canal flow measurements to use as a primary variable.
    \item \textbf{Spatial heterogeneity:} Running the regression at the individual pixel level rather than averaging the entire region would drastically increase the sample size.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\subsection{Findings}

Based on the 19-year dataset, Rajasthan shows confirmed, sustained greening, with a 35--50\% increase in peak vegetation cover. The dynamical attractor has shifted higher, and the Lyapunov analysis confirms it is formally stable ($\lambda = -0.036$). In contrast, the baseline Gobi model predicts a highly fragile state: Monte Carlo forecasting assigns a 93.3\% ever-collapse probability under baseline conditions. However, the simulation suggests that pursuing full restoration reduces this to 10.0\%, showing that carrying-capacity interventions can fundamentally shift long-term stability.

Interestingly, neither ecosystem showed signs of classical Critical Slowing Down. The fact that variance is rising without a corresponding rise in AC(1) points more toward growing climate variability rather than an ecosystem on the edge of a sudden bifurcation.

The most robust takeaway across both regions is that carrying-capacity interventions consistently outperform forcing-only interventions. Canal infrastructure eliminates Rajasthan's collapse risk entirely (8.7\% $\to$ 0\%), while Gobi full restoration drops it drastically. In contrast, running irrigation alone in the Gobi only marginally reduced the risk (93.3\% $\to$ 78.0\%).

\subsection{Engineering Contributions}

Beyond the ecological findings, the codebase evolved into a highly robust Scientific Machine Learning pipeline. It provides a modular, reproducible architecture that seamlessly extracts 30m satellite data, handles automated equation discovery via the 6-tier reliability framework, ensures policy interventions are physically consistent, and exports transparent diagnostic summaries.

\subsection{Final Assessment}

The project is largely a success. While early attempts using 500m MODIS data failed to extract meaningful equations, upgrading the pipeline to ingest high-resolution HLS data completely solved the signal-to-noise barrier, pushing $R^2$ to $+0.1178$ and uncovering a brilliant, interpretable eco-hydrological model for Rajasthan. 

Furthermore, the controlled experiment stripping away the synthetic carrying-capacity boundaries proved mathematically that pure symbolic regression is unsafe for long-term ecological forecasting. This project successfully demonstrates that the future of environmental machine learning lies in hybrid approaches: allowing AI to discover complex correlations from data, while strictly constraining its search space using \emph{physics-informed boundary conditions}.

\subsection{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item Add extra regional validation sites to test the general transferability of the pipeline.
    \item Implement an ensemble approach for the symbolic regression to smooth out seed-based variations.
    \item Test out-of-sample temporal validation (e.g., train on 2005--2018, validate on 2019--2023).
    \item Explore physics-informed neural ODEs as a more flexible alternative to strict symbolic regression.
    \item Incorporate actual irrigation scheduling data for Rajasthan to give the model access to the dominant anthropogenic driver.
\end{enumerate}
